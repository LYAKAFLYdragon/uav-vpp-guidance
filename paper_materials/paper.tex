\begin{abstract}
Trajectory prediction is widely believed to improve guidance performance in autonomous close-range tracking, yet empirical evidence for when and why prediction helps remains scarce. We train proximal policy optimization (PPO) guidance policies with three predictor configurations---no prediction, constant-velocity (CV), and constant-acceleration (CA)---from scratch on a set of feasible intercept geometries, and evaluate each on $80$ episodes under constant-velocity target motion. Surprisingly, all three configurations achieve \emph{identical} $62.5\%$ success rates with identical per-scenario success patterns. A paired $t$-test on episode return nevertheless rejects identical return distributions ($p < 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = -0.769$), showing that predictor choice affects trajectory efficiency even when it does not change terminal success. We show that CA prediction reduces mathematically to CV when target acceleration is zero ($\hat{p}_{\text{CA}} = \hat{p}_{\text{CV}}$ as $\hat{a} \to 0$), and that the no-prediction baseline can replicate the same terminal outcomes by tracking the instantaneous target position. These results imply that claims of predictor superiority must be qualified by target kinematics: under predictable motion, predictor architecture is functionally equivalent; only under maneuvering targets can advanced predictors provide measurable advantage.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Autonomous close-range tracking and intercept guidance for unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) remains a challenging problem at the intersection of aerospace control and machine learning. In terminal engagement scenarios, the pursuer must maintain a prescribed aspect angle and minimum stand-off distance while the target may execute evasive or unpredictable maneuvers. Proportional navigation and its variants have served as the workhorse of guided munitions for decades, but their performance degrades rapidly when the target dynamics deviate from the assumed kinematic model.

A natural extension is to augment the guidance loop with a \emph{trajectory predictor}: a module that forecasts the target's future position over a lookahead horizon and places a virtual point ahead of the current target location. The pursuer then tracks this virtual point rather than the instantaneous target position, ostensibly improving intercept geometry and reducing terminal error. Predictors range from simple parametric models---constant velocity (CV) or constant acceleration (CA)---to data-driven neural architectures such as long short-term memory (LSTM) and gated recurrent unit (GRU) networks.

Despite the intuitive appeal of prediction-augmented guidance, the literature offers conflicting guidance on \emph{when} prediction helps. Classical analyses show that prediction improves performance when the target follows the assumed kinematic model, but can degrade performance under model mismatch. More recent reinforcement-learning (RL) approaches train the guidance policy end-to-end with the predictor, yet rarely isolate the predictor's contribution from the policy's adaptation. The result is a gap in understanding: does prediction provide a universal benefit, or does its value depend critically on the predictor architecture, the target kinematics, and the scenario geometry?

This paper addresses that gap through a controlled empirical study. We train proximal policy optimization (PPO) guidance policies with three predictor configurations---no prediction, parametric prediction (CV/CA), and evaluate them on eight feasible intercept geometries with ten random seeds each ($80$ episodes per method), all under constant-velocity target motion. Our contributions are threefold:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We demonstrate \emph{functional equivalence}: under constant-velocity target motion, no prediction, CV prediction, and CA prediction all achieve the same $62.5\%$ success rate with identical per-scenario success patterns (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}).
    \item We show that the equivalence is \emph{kinematically expected}: when target acceleration is zero, CA's prediction equation reduces exactly to CV, and the no-prediction baseline can achieve the same terminal outcomes by tracking the instantaneous target position.
    \item We find that predictor choice nevertheless produces \emph{statistically distinct return distributions} ($p < 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = -0.769$), implying that prediction influences trajectory efficiency even when it does not change binary success.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the training pipeline, predictor architectures, scenario design, and statistical protocol. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the main quantitative findings. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the functional equivalence and its implications. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes implications and future directions.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Guidance Environment}

We formulate close-range tracking as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP). The pursuer state includes position, velocity, and attitude; the observation vector encodes relative range, line-of-sight rate, aspect angle, and target position history. The action space is three-dimensional: normal acceleration command $n_z$, roll-rate command $p$, and throttle command $\delta_t$. Episodes terminate on success (aspect angle and range constraints satisfied), crash (minimum range violated), timeout ($300$\,s elapsed), or out-of-bounds (altitude or lateral deviation exceeded).

All experiments use the \texttt{simple} backend (point-mass dynamics) to isolate predictor effects from aerodynamic modeling uncertainty. The configuration is frozen in \texttt{config/experiment/stage6f5\_feasible\_geometry.yaml}. All models were trained from scratch for $200{,}000$ PPO steps under identical hyperparameters.

\subsection{Predictor Architectures}

We evaluate three predictor configurations, detailed in Table~\ref{tab:predictors}.

\paragraph{No-Prediction} The virtual point is anchored directly on the instantaneous target position. This serves as the baseline against which all prediction-augmented methods are compared.

\paragraph{Parametric Prediction} Constant-velocity (CV) and constant-acceleration (CA) extrapolators project the target forward by a lookahead horizon of $\Delta t = 1.0$\,s. Both predictors use a history length of $5$ steps and are frozen during RL training; the policy learns to use (or ignore) their outputs.

\subsection{Training Protocol}

Each policy is trained with PPO for $200{,}000$ environment steps across $3$ random seeds. The reward function combines a sparse terminal success bonus with dense shaping terms for range minimization, aspect-angle alignment, and control effort penalties. Hyperparameters (learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, clip ratio $0.2$, GAE $\lambda = 0.95$) are fixed across all methods to ensure a fair comparison. From the three training seeds, the best-performing checkpoint is selected for the final benchmark.

Mode switching is \emph{disabled} during evaluation to prevent the policy from hiding predictor failures behind guidance-mode transitions. This conservative choice ensures that any observed effects are attributable to the predictor.

\subsection{Scenario Design}

Evaluation uses eight scenarios drawn from the feasible-geometry set (Stage~6F.5). Four are \emph{regression} scenarios used in prior work; four are \emph{candidate} scenarios designed to probe edge cases. Table~\ref{tab:scenarios} summarizes their geometries.

All scenarios use \texttt{target\_mode = constant\_velocity}. We note explicitly that this kinematic choice causes the CA predictor to degenerate mathematically to CV ($\hat{a} \to 0$). This is an expected behavior, not an implementation bug, and we report the two parametric methods as a single category to avoid a spurious distinction.

\subsection{Statistical Protocol}

Each method is evaluated on $80$ episodes ($10$ random seeds $\times$ $8$ scenarios). We report success rate (primary metric), mean episode return with standard deviation, and episode length. Statistical significance is assessed with paired $t$-tests on episode return against the no-prediction baseline, with effect size quantified by Cohen's $d$. For binary success outcomes, we use exact binomial tests where applicable. All tests use $\alpha = 0.05$ with two-sided alternatives.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Main Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:main_results} presents the overall success rates, mean returns, and statistical comparisons across the three guidance configurations.

\input{tables/table1_main_comparison.tex}

All three methods achieved identical $62.5\%$ success rates. However, a paired $t$-test on episode return rejects the null hypothesis of identical return distributions ($p < 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = -0.769$, medium effect). This shows that predictor choice influences trajectory efficiency---episode length, control effort, and terminal reward shaping---even when it does not change whether the terminal constraints are satisfied.

\subsection{Per-Scenario Breakdown}

Table~\ref{tab:per_scenario} decomposes the overall success rate by scenario.

\input{tables/table_per_scenario.tex}

All three methods produced \emph{identical} per-scenario success patterns. All methods succeeded on crossing challenging, crossing left, crossing right, head-on close, and crossing close scenarios ($100\%$ each). All methods failed on head-on neutral, head-on medium, and head-on far ($0\%$ each). The failures on head-on neutral, medium, and far suggest these geometries lie outside the stable training distribution or violate the guidance constraints under the current initialization protocol.

The identical per-scenario pattern is the central empirical result of this study: under constant-velocity target motion, the predictor architecture does not change the set of scenarios in which the policy succeeds or fails.

\subsection{Return Distribution Analysis}

Despite identical success rates, the mean returns differ slightly: no-prediction ($-113.64 \pm 403.78$) versus parametric prediction ($-114.24 \pm 404.44$). The paired $t$-test detects this difference with high confidence ($p < 0.0001$) and a medium effect size ($d = -0.769$). The difference is small in absolute terms---approximately $0.6$ return units per episode on average---but is consistent across the $80$ paired episodes. This implies that prediction introduces small, systematic changes to trajectory efficiency that do not alter binary outcomes under the tested kinematics.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We conducted a controlled comparison of three predictor architectures for UAV close-range tracking guidance under constant-velocity target motion. Contrary to the intuition that prediction universally improves performance, we find that all three configurations---no prediction, constant-velocity prediction, and constant-acceleration prediction---achieve identical $62.5\%$ success rates with identical per-scenario success patterns. The equivalence is kinematically expected: CA reduces to CV when target acceleration is zero, and the no-prediction baseline can replicate the same terminal outcomes by tracking the instantaneous target position. Predictor choice nevertheless produces statistically distinct return distributions ($p < 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = -0.769$), showing that prediction influences trajectory efficiency even when it does not change binary success. These results caution against blanket adoption of prediction-augmented guidance and highlight the need for target-kinematics-aware evaluation in autonomous aerial systems.
