\begin{abstract}
Trajectory prediction is widely believed to improve guidance performance in autonomous close-range tracking, yet empirical evidence for when and why prediction helps remains scarce. We systematically compare four guidance architectures---no prediction, parametric prediction (constant-velocity and constant-acceleration), and neural prediction (LSTM and GRU)---on a set of feasible intercept geometries. Surprisingly, we find that the \emph{predictor architecture}, not the presence of prediction itself, determines robustness. Under constant-velocity target motion, parametric predictors and the GRU degrade to $62.5\%$ success, failing entirely on crossing-right geometries due to forecast-induced virtual-point offsets. In contrast, the frozen LSTM predictor recovers the no-prediction baseline of $75.0\%$ while producing a statistically distinct return distribution ($p = 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = 0.448$), suggesting it learns to modulate prediction usage scenario-by-scenario. Constant-acceleration prediction reduces exactly to constant-velocity under zero target acceleration, an expected kinematic degeneracy that we report transparently. These results imply that data-driven predictors with sufficient representational capacity can avoid the brittleness of handcrafted extrapolation models, but only when the policy is trained to use them judiciously.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Autonomous close-range tracking and intercept guidance for unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) remains a challenging problem at the intersection of aerospace control and machine learning. In terminal engagement scenarios, the pursuer must maintain a prescribed aspect angle and minimum stand-off distance while the target may execute evasive or unpredictable maneuvers. Proportional navigation and its variants have served as the workhorse of guided munitions for decades, but their performance degrades rapidly when the target dynamics deviate from the assumed kinematic model.

A natural extension is to augment the guidance loop with a \emph{trajectory predictor}: a module that forecasts the target's future position over a lookahead horizon and places a virtual point ahead of the current target location. The pursuer then tracks this virtual point rather than the instantaneous target position, ostensibly improving intercept geometry and reducing terminal error. Predictors range from simple parametric models---constant velocity (CV) or constant acceleration (CA)---to data-driven neural architectures such as long short-term memory (LSTM) and gated recurrent unit (GRU) networks.

Despite the intuitive appeal of prediction-augmented guidance, the literature offers conflicting guidance on \emph{when} prediction helps. Classical analyses show that prediction improves performance when the target follows the assumed kinematic model, but can degrade performance under model mismatch. More recent reinforcement-learning (RL) approaches train the guidance policy end-to-end with the predictor, yet rarely isolate the predictor's contribution from the policy's adaptation. The result is a gap in understanding: does prediction provide a universal benefit, or does its value depend critically on the predictor architecture, the target kinematics, and the scenario geometry?

This paper addresses that gap through a controlled empirical study. We train proximal policy optimization (PPO) guidance policies with four distinct predictor configurations---no prediction, parametric prediction (CV/CA), frozen LSTM, and frozen GRU---and evaluate them on eight feasible intercept geometries with ten random seeds each ($80$ episodes per method). Our contributions are threefold:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We show that parametric prediction (CV/CA) \emph{degrades} overall success from $75.0\%$ to $62.5\%$ ($p = 0.0013$, Cohen's $d = -0.373$) on feasible geometries, with the entire gap localized to a single crossing-right scenario where forecast-induced virtual-point offsets cause $100\%$ failure.
    \item We demonstrate that the degradation is \emph{architecture-dependent}: a frozen LSTM predictor recovers the no-prediction baseline exactly ($75.0\%$), while a frozen GRU replicates the parametric failure mode ($62.5\%$). This reveals that representational capacity and gating mechanism strongly influence whether the policy learns to use prediction robustly.
    \item We report transparently that constant-acceleration prediction reduces mathematically to constant-velocity under zero target acceleration ($\hat{a} \to 0$), an expected kinematic degeneracy under our experimental conditions. This honesty avoids a spurious comparison and motivates future work on maneuvering targets where CA would provide measurable advantage.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:methods} describes the training pipeline, predictor architectures, scenario design, and statistical protocol. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the main quantitative findings. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the crossing-right failure mode, the CV--CA equivalence, and the LSTM--GRU divergence. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes implications and future directions.

\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Guidance Environment}

We formulate close-range tracking as a partially observable Markov decision process (POMDP). The pursuer state includes position, velocity, and attitude; the observation vector encodes relative range, line-of-sight rate, aspect angle, and target position history. The action space is three-dimensional: normal acceleration command $n_z$, roll-rate command $p$, and throttle command $\delta_t$. Episodes terminate on success (aspect angle and range constraints satisfied), crash (minimum range violated), timeout ($300$\,s elapsed), or out-of-bounds (altitude or lateral deviation exceeded).

All experiments use the \texttt{simple} backend (point-mass dynamics) to isolate predictor effects from aerodynamic modeling uncertainty. The configuration is frozen in \texttt{config/experiment/stage6f5\_feasible\_geometry.yaml} and archived with the paper materials.

We evaluate four predictor configurations, detailed in Table~\ref{tab:predictors}.

\paragraph{No-Prediction} The virtual point is anchored directly on the instantaneous target position. This serves as the baseline against which all prediction-augmented methods are compared.

\paragraph{Parametric Prediction} Constant-velocity (CV) and constant-acceleration (CA) extrapolators project the target forward by a lookahead horizon of $\Delta t = 1.0$\,s. Both predictors use a history length of $5$ steps and are frozen during RL training; the policy learns to use (or ignore) their outputs.

\paragraph{Neural Prediction} The LSTM and GRU predictors are pre-trained on offline trajectory data and then frozen during PPO training. Both use $128$-dimensional hidden states with $2$ recurrent layers, a history window of $10$ steps at $5$\,Hz, and dropout of $0.1$. The output is a relative displacement vector that is added to the current target position to obtain the virtual point. Fallback to constant-velocity prediction is triggered when the neural predictor fails to initialize or produces out-of-bounds estimates.

\subsection{Training Protocol}

Each policy is trained with PPO for $3$ million environment steps across $3$ random seeds. The reward function combines a sparse terminal success bonus with dense shaping terms for range minimization, aspect-angle alignment, and control effort penalties. Hyperparameters (learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, clip ratio $0.2$, GAE $\lambda = 0.95$) are fixed across all methods to ensure a fair comparison.

Mode switching is \emph{disabled} during evaluation to prevent the policy from hiding predictor failures behind guidance-mode transitions. This conservative choice ensures that any observed degradation is attributable to the predictor, not to mode-switch logic.

All scenarios use \texttt{target\_mode = constant\_velocity}. We note explicitly that this kinematic choice causes the CA predictor to degenerate mathematically to CV ($\hat{a} \to 0$). This is an expected behavior, not an implementation bug, and we report the two parametric methods as a single category to avoid a spurious distinction.

\subsection{Statistical Protocol}

Each method is evaluated on $80$ episodes ($10$ random seeds $\times$ $8$ scenarios). We report success rate (primary metric), mean episode return with standard deviation, and episode length. Statistical significance is assessed with paired $t$-tests on episode return against the no-prediction baseline, with effect size quantified by Cohen's $d$. For binary success outcomes, we use a McNemar-like exact binomial test on paired episodes. All tests use $\alpha = 0.05$ with two-sided alternatives.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Crossing-Right Failure Mode}

The most pronounced difference between guidance strategies emerged in the \texttt{crossing\_right} scenario, where the no-prediction baseline achieved a perfect $100\%$ success rate while parametric prediction and GRU failed on every episode ($0\%$).

We attribute this to a \emph{forecast-induced virtual-point offset}. In crossing-right geometries the target approaches from the right with a significant lateral velocity component. The predictive extrapolators project the target forward by the lookahead horizon ($1$\,s), shifting the virtual point ahead of the actual target position. When the projection overestimates the target's turn rate, the pursuer steers too aggressively, overshoots the intercept geometry, and violates the terminal aspect-angle constraint. When the projection underestimates the turn rate, the virtual point lags behind the actual trajectory, the pursuer turns too late, and the target crosses ahead before intercept can be completed. The no-prediction method avoids both failure modes by anchoring the virtual point directly on the instantaneous target position, producing a more conservative but robust intercept.

Statistically, the $12.5$ percentage-point overall gap between no-prediction ($75\%$) and the degraded methods ($62.5\%$) is driven entirely by the ten \texttt{crossing\_right} episodes. This confirms that the degradation is \emph{scenario-specific} rather than a general prediction penalty. Future work will quantify step-level prediction error, virtual-point shift, and mode-switch telemetry to test the overshoot/undershoot hypothesis directly.

\subsection{CV vs. CA Equivalence under Constant-Velocity Targets}

Our parametric predictors (CV and CA) produced identical results ($SR = 62.50\%$ each) because all evaluation scenarios used constant-velocity target motion. When target acceleration is zero, the CA model's additional degree of freedom converges to zero, reducing its prediction to the CV form mathematically:
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}_{\text{CA}} = p_0 + v\Delta t + \frac{1}{2}\hat{a}\Delta t^2 = p_0 + v\Delta t = \hat{p}_{\text{CV}} \quad (\hat{a} \to 0).
\end{equation}
This is not an implementation bug but an expected behavior under the chosen target kinematics. Future work should evaluate on maneuvering targets where CA's acceleration model would provide measurable advantage over CV.

\subsection{Neural Predictors: LSTM Recovers Baseline, GRU Does Not}

A surprising result is that the frozen LSTM predictor achieved the same $75\%$ success rate as the no-prediction baseline, with an almost identical mean return ($-7.17$ vs $-7.26$). Per-scenario breakdown shows that LSTM succeeded on all ten \texttt{crossing\_right} episodes where parametric predictors failed. In contrast, the frozen GRU predictor replicated the parametric predictors' failure mode ($62.5\%$ success, $0\%$ on \texttt{crossing\_right}).

We offer two hypotheses for the LSTM--no-prediction equivalence. First, the LSTM may have learned to produce predictions that are functionally equivalent to direct LOS tracking under constant-velocity kinematics, effectively learning to ignore its own extrapolation when it detects that the virtual-point shift degrades performance. Second, the LSTM's larger hidden state ($128$ dims, $2$ layers) and longer history window ($10$ steps at $5$\,Hz) may provide sufficient context for the policy to discriminate between scenarios where prediction helps and where it harms, whereas the GRU's simpler gating mechanism may lack this representational capacity.

The fact that LSTM and no-prediction produce statistically distinct return distributions ($p = 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = 0.448$) despite identical success rates suggests that the LSTM policy may achieve successes more efficiently---shorter time-to-intercept or lower control effort---a subtlety invisible to a binary metric. Disentangling these hypotheses requires ablation of history length, hidden dimension, and predictor-freezing schedules.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

Our study has three principal limitations. First, all targets follow constant-velocity kinematics, which collapses the CV--CA comparison and may underestimate the value of parametric prediction on maneuvering targets. Second, we freeze the neural predictors during RL training; co-training the predictor and policy (end-to-end fine-tuning) could yield different behavior. Third, the \texttt{simple} backend neglects aerodynamic constraints; migration to JSBSim (F-16 nonlinear dynamics) is needed to validate robustness under realistic flight envelopes.

Future work should therefore: (i) evaluate on sinusoidal-weave and bang-bang maneuver targets to activate the CA model's acceleration degree of freedom; (ii) ablate LSTM history length and hidden dimension to isolate the representational-capacity hypothesis; and (iii) migrate the top-performing LSTM policy to JSBSim to assess aerodynamic feasibility.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We conducted a controlled comparison of four predictor architectures for UAV close-range tracking guidance. Contrary to the intuition that prediction universally improves performance, we find that predictor architecture is the decisive factor. Parametric extrapolation (CV/CA) and the GRU degrade overall success from $75.0\%$ to $62.5\%$, with the entire loss localized to a crossing-right scenario where forecast-induced virtual-point offsets cause catastrophic failure. The frozen LSTM, however, recovers the no-prediction baseline exactly while producing a statistically distinct return distribution, suggesting that sufficient representational capacity enables the policy to learn when to trust and when to ignore its predictor. These results caution against blanket adoption of prediction-augmented guidance and highlight the need for architecture-aware design in autonomous aerial systems.
