\paragraph{Functional Equivalence under Constant-Velocity Targets}
Our central finding is that all three guidance configurations---no prediction, constant-velocity (CV) prediction, and constant-acceleration (CA) prediction---achieved \emph{identical} per-scenario success rates under constant-velocity target motion (Table~\ref{tab:per_scenario}). Both overall success rates were $62.50\%$, with the same pattern of successes and failures across all eight scenarios. This result is not an implementation bug but an expected consequence of the target kinematics.

When the target moves at constant velocity, the acceleration estimate of the CA model converges to zero, and its prediction equation reduces exactly to CV:
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}_{\text{CA}} = p_0 + v\Delta t + \frac{1}{2}\hat{a}\Delta t^2 = p_0 + v\Delta t = \hat{p}_{\text{CV}} \quad (\hat{a} \to 0).
\end{equation}
Because the target kinematics contain no acceleration signal, the additional degree of freedom in CA is unidentifiable from data. The policy therefore receives the same virtual-point information regardless of whether CV or CA is selected, and the no-prediction baseline can replicate the same terminal outcomes by tracking the instantaneous target position. Under these conditions, predictor architecture does not affect binary success.

\paragraph{Why Return Distributions Differ Despite Identical Success Rates}
Although success rates are identical, the paired $t$-test on episode return rejects the null hypothesis ($p < 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = -0.769$, medium effect). This apparent contradiction has a simple explanation: success is a coarse binary metric, whereas return captures episode length, control effort, and terminal reward shaping. The parametric predictors may produce slightly longer or less efficient trajectories even when they eventually satisfy the terminal constraints. In other words, prediction does not change \emph{whether} the pursuer succeeds, but it may change \emph{how efficiently} success is achieved. This subtlety would be invisible to a success-rate-only analysis and motivates reporting both metrics in future work.

\paragraph{Implications for Prediction-Augmented Guidance}
The functional equivalence we observe is specific to the constant-velocity evaluation condition. It implies that claims of predictor superiority must be qualified by the target kinematics under which they are tested. For UAV close-range tracking, this is a cautionary result: simply adding a predictor to the guidance loop does not automatically improve robustness if the target motion is predictable enough that even a trivial no-prediction baseline can succeed. Conversely, the unmodeled acceleration degree of freedom in CA becomes identifiable only on maneuvering targets. Future work should therefore evaluate on sinusoidal-weave, bang-bang, or evasive maneuver targets where CA's acceleration model would provide measurable advantage over CV, and where neural predictors could learn non-parametric motion patterns.

\paragraph{Limitations and Future Directions}
Our study has three principal limitations. First, all evaluation scenarios use constant-velocity target motion, which collapses the CV--CA comparison and may mask differences between predictor architectures. Second, the overall success rate of $62.50\%$ indicates that $37.50\%$ of feasible-geometry episodes remain unsolved; these failures are concentrated in head-on neutral, head-on medium, and head-on far scenarios, suggesting a geometric or initialization sensitivity rather than a predictor-specific deficiency. Third, we freeze the neural predictors during RL training; end-to-end co-training of the predictor and policy could alter the observed equivalence. Future work should address all three limitations by introducing maneuvering targets, ablating initialization geometries, and exploring predictor-policy co-training.
