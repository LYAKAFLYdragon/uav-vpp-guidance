On the feasible-geometry benchmark (eight scenarios, ten random seeds, $80$ episodes per method), all three guidance configurations achieved the same $62.5\%$ success rate. No-prediction, constant-velocity prediction, and constant-acceleration prediction produced identical per-scenario success patterns: $100\%$ on crossing challenging, crossing left, crossing right, head-on close, and crossing close; $0\%$ on head-on neutral, head-on medium, and head-on far. A paired $t$-test on episode return nevertheless rejects the null hypothesis of identical return distributions ($p < 0.0001$, Cohen's $d = -0.769$), indicating that prediction influences trajectory efficiency even when it does not change terminal success. The constant-acceleration predictor reduced mathematically to constant-velocity under zero target acceleration, confirming that the observed equivalence is kinematically expected rather than an implementation artifact.
